Detecting and Correcting the Parts That Trigger Photosensitive Epilepsy on Video with Image Processing Methods

Ahmet Yasir Kilic, Mustafa Can Genc, Ali Kural

Introduction

  Photosensitive epilepsy is a condition that affects 1 in 100 people worldwide, and 3% of people with epilepsy have photosensitive epilepsy. This project aims to create an interface that enables people to consume digital visual content more safely. Image processing methods will be used to investigate the sections that trigger photosensitive epilepsy. Algorithms will be developed and a correction approach will be tried. After the harmful video segments that may trigger epilepsy are detected, the necessary information will be given to the correction tool and the success of the correction will be measured.

Problem Definition and Literature Review

 Photosensitive Epilepsy

 People of all ages can suffer from epilepsy, a neurological disorder that often first manifests in children. It can result in loss of consciousness, uncontrolled movements, and possibly fatal brain injury. A kind of epilepsy called photosensitive epilepsy (PSE) is brought on by contrasting or flashing patterns. There are millions of people who are believed to be affected by it, according to various estimates. Although triggers can be discovered in the natural world, they are most frequently connected to digital screens.
 Visual material that might cause photosensitive epilepsy can also make healthy individuals uncomfortable. The most crucial information is that epilepsy may strike anybody at any age, and that flashing lights and contrasting patterns can make people feel ill, dizzy, and uncomfortable. Strong photosensitivity triggers may affect healthy people in both good and bad ways, therefore it's crucial for everyone to be able to avoid them.

 Incidents

 There is a well konwn incident that 685 people were directly affected by a scene in the 38th episode of the 1st season of the cartoon Pokemon, which was broadcast in Japan in 1997, and applied to the emergency services after having an epileptic seizure.

BBC News report on the banned Pok√©mon episode which caused seizures

 Liana Ruppert, an associate editor at Game Informer, experienced a severe grand seizure while playing "Cyberpunk 2077" due to a sequence in the game with rapid flashing lights. Ruppert authored an article about her experience, leading to international media attention and prompting the game's developer, CD Projekt Red, to add a seizure warning and make adjustments to the game to remove seizure triggers. The incident highlighted the need for greater awareness and consideration of photosensitive epilepsy in video games and other forms of visual entertainment.
